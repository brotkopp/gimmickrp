GimmickRP Skirmish

Preface

GimmickRP is a rule set for pen and paper role playing games. This is the skirmish variant. I do not bother to explain what that means, because others already did. Or at least I thought so before I started googling. Anyway, I will not waste time on this right now. Since all rule books I recently read are written in English, I somehow feel urged to do so, too. Plus it makes gimmick names sound catchier in most cases.

About the Design

I copied the following sequence in this place because i like it and it may become handy one day for the preface. Originally i started the team setup section with it in order to emphasize that the model features are taken care of by the rules in quite a rough manner:

There is a big variety of miniature models out there and even more stuff people actually play skirmish games with (e.g. Lego). There are a bunch of setting stereotypes and hardly anyone does not do some crossover to make things even more diverse. There are things like trenches, phalanxes, magic, hackers, drones, undead, orbital reconnaissance and there is certainly a big difference between a bayonet and a gladius... but in the end we are just shoving tiny figures around the table shouting “Bang! You’re dead!” Let’s not take this too seriously.[a]

Gimmicks

All visible gear and other relevant features of the miniature should be advantage neutral and available at level 0 since they should be picked according to the model’s appearance which is quite static since I assume that this rule set most of the time is not played with LEGO.

Basically the “advantage neutral visible model gimmick” approach has two goals:

  1. It should relieve the player of the burden to have to balance the model’s gimmicks.
  2. It should narrow down the number of gimmicks you need to care about in the beginning.

The disadvantage is a lack of flexibility. Moreover, by trying to balance every single starter gimmick, they become bulky and cumbersome, and maybe even more complex than the alternative, which would be to stick to the old tier 0 only gimmicks and leave the balance to the player.

So a better way to reduce complexity for beginners could be to provide prototype setups. Maybe only 4 of the most common types of models will suffice to give the player an idea, where to start.

The next thing i am going to try is to redesign the gimmick list to just consist of gimmicks.

New approach: Return of the advantage neutral gimmicks but not as gimmicks. Parts of the rule book may refer to rough model features and thus implementing advantage neutral gimmicks on base level. Why is this cool? Because someone who wants to get started does not have to read the gimmick list.

Action Sequence

One goal of designing the combat mechanics is to have as least as possible markers floating around the battlefield, since shoving them around with the models is tiresome and a source of havoc and confusion (“Whom in this melee bunch do these activation markers belong to again?”). Another goal is to maintain the focus on dramatically meaningful actions. I want to get away from the illusion that we are handling all action mechanically possible in a fixed period of time just because this seems like the most accurate way to simulate combat. It is completely feasible that some models are standing by for quite some turns just because they are not involved in what is happening. This might not be 100% realistic but it is sufficiently plausible and it is closer to the manner action movies tell their stories. After all, this is about fun and not science.

Old AP rules (archive)

The cost of an action basically calculates as follows: The first action of each model comes at the cost of 1 AP. The second action of each model costs 2 AP, the third 3 AP (and so on, more actions may be possible, but are unlikely, especially at the beginning). For example one player rolls a 3. Then he or she may perform 3 actions with 3 different models (each at the cost of 1 AP), or 2 actions with one model (1 AP for the first and 2 AP for the second). The amount of AP involved in one turn should be low enough to be kept track of without throwing markers at the board.

Movement

After watching the tabletop basement’s introduction video to their Deadzone feature week, I realized what I loved most about Mantic’s Deadzone and what is completely not featured in this ruleset: Dead simple movement rules.

If i could just copy those, i would, but i can not for the following reasons:

  1. I aim to be flexible towards the assets used to play this game so i can not impose the quantization of the battlefield into 3 inch cubes like Deadzone does.
  2. I do not have a fixed precalculated set of units and so i need more flexibility with stats than Deadzone does for balance (on a second thought, this system is reduced in respect of damage pretty much like dead zone is reduced in respect of movement).
  3. I do not aim to copy the action sequence model of Deadzone since that would include the use of markers.

Still, there is a lot of room for improvement from what i currently got (success value based movement) to something that just makes sense to anybody right away without players getting tangled up in ambiguous calculations with no relevant outcome.

Another thought pointing into the same direction: After redesigning movement to a fixed range with checks for obstacles in between I still feel like breaking the flow of a single action by interrupting it to let the player roll in order to determine if he or she can go on. This might lead to funny surprises like “Oh, damn! Now my model got stuck half way to its destination! What an unexpected twist! This is gonna be interesting!”. This scenario is more likely to unfold the way that players are haunted by the possibility of not finishing their model’s movement and thus are slowed in their planning. Further questioning these mechanics: How many obstacles are there gonna be on a 6 inch path? Most likely there will be one piece of cover in the beginning of the trail and that is about it. Making the player roll for that obstacle and by chance cancel the whole movement is probably not funny at all. As much as I like my checks because I can pile bonuses on them without breaking anything I commit myself to the following dogma: Movement (including climbing) should be predictable.

Combat Mechanics

The combat mechanics are those that regulate the movement of the miniatures and the actions we imagine them to perform on the table. Before we start with that, there is one core mechanic to elaborate first.

Dice Checks

Dice checks are mainly used to evaluate the outcome of some action. To do so the player basically rolls a 6 sided die and if the result of that roll is higher than some difficulty value, the action is considered a success. There are some variants of this mechanic that add some complexity.

Whatever Check +n or -n

Occasionally some condition, or gimmick, or even several of them might state that there is a +1 or -1 on a certain check (e.g. melee check). Those pluses and minuses are added up until you have a total +n or -n or zero. Zero means that the player is going to roll one die - just as described above. A +n means that the player is going to roll 1 + n dice and pick the highest result. And corresponding to that -n means that the player is going to roll 1 + n dice but has to pick the lowest result.

Difficulty and Interference

In order to perform successfully the player has to roll a result higher than any difficulty and any interference. These may be several different values but they do not add up! Thus only the highest of them is relevant. For dramatical reasons we might distinguish between difficulty and interference, albeit the maths are the same.

Difficulties are constant values dictated by the rules of the check performed. They are known before the check is rolled and they may even lead to the decision not to perform the action in question at all. One difficulty for example is the protection level of the target, which applies to all attacks.

Interference is a concurring action of another model trying to prevent or even counteract the action in question. Interferences are checks themselves and are performed after the player rolled the dice for the check in question. For example: Model a is trying to run past model b while model b is trying to stop model a. The players of both models perform the according checks the one with the higher result is successful.

Action Sequence

Round

A round is a sequence in which every player takes one turn. At the beginning of a round each player rolls an AP check for his team. So this is the first check and probably the most irregular one: There is no difficulty or interference at all, it is all about the success value. So basically the result of that roll counts. Modifiers might take effect, like doing a “+1 AP check” if any condition says so. That is why we are talking about a check at all. This check determines two thing:

  1. The order in which the players take their turns. Starting with the player with the lowest (!) result, the players take their turns in ascending order.
  2. The number of action points (AP) the player may spend in his or her turn is equal to the success value of that check.[b][c]

Those that fear that bad luck with AP checks will leave them as spectators of their team’s demise might find solace in knowing that the designer is quite aware of this problem and wants to point out two aspects of these rules:

  • The player with the lowest result starts the round. That does not actually give him or her an advantage that might be expressed in a spreadsheet but he or she is in the position of vaguely knowing what is coming for him or her and he or she has an opportunity to prepare.
  • When one player takes his or her turn and has a lot of AP to spend, that does not mean that the other player’s models stand around like statues. While the models take action, enemy models often have the opportunity to react. Hence all the talk about interference. More about that in the types of action section.

Turn

During his  or her turn the player may spend AP to let the models perform several actions (described further down). An action generally comes at the cost of 1 AP if not stated otherwise (see climbing and complex actions). A single model may perform a limited number of actions per turn.

Types of Action

So here comes what a model can do when the player wants it to do something. Every action may potentially be reacted to by enemy models resulting in the interference described in the dice check section. The described actions and reactions are the default set of actions any model may perform. It may be explicitly altered by circumstances or gimmicks. For example, while most models can not do a thing about being shot at, a model with the gimmick “Pistolero” may react by shooting back.

Movement Action

A model may choose to move up to its movement range (referred to as MR - by default 4) in movement units. The movement unit of a model is roughly the distance between the sole of one of its feet and the top of the head. This may sound a bit cumbersome but it reflects the speed of both - upright walking and a four legged manner of movement. While performing this movement, it may overcome 1 obstacle. Obstacles are terrain features with a height from half to a full movement unit. A model may as part of its movement jump down any heights. If it jumps down deeper than two movement units it goes down.

When moving through hempering ground conditions like ruble, water or riddled structures, movement is halved.

Any enemy model may react when the moving model is about to move into melee range at some point on the track [d][e][f][g][h]with a melee attack by performing an interfering melee check (inflicting no damage if successful, just terminating the movement).

Melee Action

The model may engage in melee by attacking an opponent that is in melee range. Melee range is considered to be something between base to base contact and some millimeters that occur when shoving the models around on the table.

The target of that melee action may react with a melee counter attack by performing an interfering melee check (inflicting damage if successful).

Ranged Action

The model might perform an attack by range on any model which it has line of sight to. The ranged check usually has just the protection level PL as difficulty. The model must have a ranged weapon in hand in order to perform such an attack.

Any opposing model in melee range of the acting model may react with a melee attack by performing an interfering melee check (inflicting damage if successful).

Funny thing about the 12 inch malus: 12 inch is quite close to the long side of the A4 format so you may use any A4 print medium as a measuring tool.

Recovery Action

When gone down (see below), the only action that a model may perform is to get back on its feet – in other words to recover. The difficulty of a recovery check is 3, if not stated otherwise.

Any opposing model in melee range of the acting model may react with a melee attack by performing an interfering melee check (inflicting damage if successful).

Help Some Teammate up

Put some teammate in melee range back on its feet without check. Enemies may interfere with melee, you may defend with melee.[i]

Climb Action

A model standing right next to a structure that is higher than the model itself may climb the structure. Climbing costs as much AP as by the times the structure’s height exceeds the model’s height. The climbing always goes as far as to the next position where the model might be put standing. In respect of interference climbing works just like a complex action does.

Complex Action

This is meant as a collective term for all mission relevant actions like hacking a computer, planting explosives, looting a container and such. Complex actions cost 2 additional AP and fail automatically if some opponent interferes.

Any opposing model in melee range of the acting model may react with a melee attack by performing an interfering melee check (inflicting damage if successful).

Damage

An attack succeeds if its result is higher than any interfering check and also higher than the target's protection level PL (which is 3 by default). Usually a successful attack results in a regular hit and - unless stated otherwise - the target “goes down”. The model is laid down to illustrate that state. A model that has gone down…

  • … is regarded as out of action when taking damage again and taken off the board.
  • ... may not perform any action except recovery.
  • … may not react.
  • … may be brought back to its feet by others helping it up.

Some hits are described to be “heavy hits”, meaning that the target is considered as out of action right away.

A model that has gone “out of action” is taken from the board and will most likely not come back during the current battle.

Fleeing

A model that leaves the board by movement is considered to have fled.

Team setup

A team consists of 6 regular size models. You might prepare a team sheet that lists its members. We recommend doing so and giving them names especially in campaign mode.

Basic Model Features

This wraps up the effects described in the boxes accompanying the previous section.

Big models

  • take up 2 regular model’s team space
  • may ignore incoming hits by spending AP
  • always deal heavy hits in melee
  • give the attacker +1 when targeted by a ranged attack

Small models

  • take up 0.5 regular model’s team space
  • suffer -1 on melee checks
  • always suffer heavy hits
  • give the attacker -1 when targeted by a ranged attack

Models Wearing a Significant Amount of Armour

  • have PL 4
  • may perform only one action per turn

Models that are dressed lightly

  • have PL 2
  • may perform up to three actions per turn

Assigning Gimmicks

Chances are that the basic model features do not sufficiently describe what you think your model can do. Therefore the gimmicks are provided. There are 2 types of gimmicks that are at this point especially interesting in respect of their effect on the team balance rating TBR.

Powerup gimmicks provide advantages to the model or the whole team with bonuses or by expanding the player’s options. Each powerup adds 1 to TBR.

Weakness gimmicks make the model suffer with lowered stats or less combat options. Each weakness reduces the TBR by 1.

So each player may assign gimmicks to his or her team as he or she pleases given that in the end their TBR does not exceed the agreed upon value which the author suggests to be 0 for starters[j]. Players may agree on any value they like but it might crash the resources of this game theoretically at the point where it exceeds 6 (team size) x 2 (if small) x total number of powerups which is practically far beyond absurd[k].

Any gimmick may be assigned during setup.

Bigger Teams

If you wish to play with bigger numbers of models you could just increase the team size if it weren’t for the initiative mechanics. The rules may be able to tackle smaller deviations as they might occur through model sizes. But if you for example double the team size things might get awkward. Therefore the author suggests setting up multiple allied teams instead. In case of campaign mode those teams share a team sheet.

Battle

The battlefield setup is performed in different phases that have a fixed order:

  1. Setting up teams as described in the previous section.
  2. Assign the space of the battle ground meaning the piece of table you want to play on. Define the features that mark the border.
  3. Objectives
  4. Positioning of teams and targets
  5. Place some structures basically to block ways and lines of sight.
  6. Place some obstacles for cover or as stairs.

Objectives

In order to determine a team’s objective, the player rolls on the objective table.[l]

roll

label

description

1

Keep them occupied

The operation is merely a distraction to keep enemy forces from more urgent matters. The team wins when the battle lasts 8 rounds.

2

Anti material operation

The team wants to demolish some crucial structures or hack some terminals. 3 targets are placed on the battlefield. The team wins once it successfully performs a complex action at 2 of them.

3

Loot run

The team is out for resources and riches! Technically it works like the anti material operation only that the models performing the complex actions also need to move off the battlefield.

4

Break through

The team is on the run! The team wins once 4 of its members[m] have fled across the edge not further than 6 inches from the enemy's spawn position.

5

Capture

The team is out for some ransom, someone to interrogate or to take to court. The team wins if it manages to perform a complex action in melee range of an enemy model thus taking it into custody and then move off the battlefield with that very model which performed that complex action.

6

Assasination

The team’s goal is to take out their worst enemy. The team wins if it takes that enemy model out of action which has the highest TBR. If there are multiple models with the same TBR determine the target by random. The target is actually dead if the team succeeds.

Positioning of Teams and Targets

A player places its team’s models in a diameter of 3 inches around the spawn position at the beginning of battle. Each team’s spawn position is placed in a corner of the battlefield as far as possible from the others.

Targets are placed around the middle of the battle ground with roughly 9 inches distance between each other.

Battle Resolution

A battle ends when there is only one team left on the battlefield rendering that team the winner of the battle. A battle may end earlier whenever a team fulfills its objective, in which case that team is the winner.

Campaign Mode

Up to this point all rules handle a one shot session starting from a collection of miniatures, battle ground assets and empty sheets of paper (maybe printed with some rooster table). The campaign mode will link the single battles. It starts with a team setup and a battle setup just like any one shot would plus that they now definitely should make use of a team sheet containing the following information:

  • Every member of the team, meaning
  • Name
  • Short description (or photograph of the model)
  • Gimmicks
  • State (active, available, occupied)
  • Recruitment TBR (see further down)
  • Official score
  • Rogue score

From there on things start just like a one shot would. The differences occur between the battles to come. The first thing is that the outcome of the battle has more elaborate consequences than just designating a winner.

Progress and Survival

For every model still alive and on the board and on its feet at the end of a battle roll an unmodified progress check. For every model fled or gone down in the winning team roll a progress check -1. For every progress check find the effect by result in the following table.

Result

Effect

1

The model has suffered but also grown on the challenge. Thus it gains 1 weakness gimmick and 1 powerup gimmick.

2

The model is exhausted and needs a break for one session

3

The model is glad to be back home safely!

4

The model earned a new veteran powerup gimmick, if its TBR is 0 or lower

5

The model earned a new veteran powerup gimmick, if its TBR is 1 or lower

6

The model earned a new veteran powerup gimmick, if its TBR is 2 or lower

For each model out of action make an unmodified survival check. For every model on the loser team that has fled or is down at the end of battle roll a survival check +1. For every survival check find the effect by result in the following table.

Result

Effect

1

The model died on the battlefield or has gone missing permanently.

2

Though the model is brought to safety, it succumbs to its wounds. But its death is not in vain! It manages to pass on some viable knowledge, so that it may pass on a veteran powerup gimmick to another team member! (if it has had any)

3

The model is missing for one session, then rolls on the loser table again.

4

The model takes permanent damage in the form of a weakness gimmick.

5

The model is badly injured and will need the next session to heal.

6

The model was lucky and escaped without any damage or restrictions.

Official Score

When a team wins a battle it gains one point official score.

  • A player may spend one point of official score to assign a new veteran powerup gimmick to one of its models.
  • A player may spend official score to increase the recruitment TBR (which is 0) for one model by 1 for each point spent.
  • A player may spend one point of official score to recruit beyond a team member count of 6.

Rogue Score

When a team loses a battle (meaning it does not win) it gains one point rogue score.

  • A player may spend rogue score points to roll up an additional objective for each point spent.
  • One point of rogue score may also be spent to remove a model from the team.

Before the Next Battle

In campaign mode a player sticks with his or her team - more or less. As an effect of the last battle some models might be not available - sometimes permanently. Or a player decides that his or her team has enough support (e.g. official score) to train some new members for harsh times and send some veterans on vacation.

A player may recruit models up to the team member count of 6 without any cost. For recruiting further models the player has to spend one point official score each. If the player fancies to recruit a model whose TBR exceeds the recruitment TBR of 0, he or she has to pay official score by the amount of the TBR exceeded.

Playing with Cards

Playing with cards is designed optional since this is a free rule set and not everybody might have the means or the will to print cards. Thus some gimmicks are marked as requiring cards. If the players decide, to play without cards, they skip those.
